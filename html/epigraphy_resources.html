1Text corpora & databases

The authoritative places to find the Greek text of an inscription.

  • PHI Greek Inscriptions (Packard Humanities Institute) PERSONAL The field standard: ~210,000 searchable Greek inscription texts, Cornell + Ohio State + PHI. License is personal study only, no copies beyond personal use — which is exactly your case. Your primary text source. Landing page: packhum.org/texts.
  • Inscriptiones Graecae (IG) — Berlin-Brandenburg Academy FREE / PD SUBSCRIPTION The great print corpus (successor to the 1815 Corpus Inscriptionum Graecarum), edited region by region. Older volumes are public domain (HathiTrust / archive.org); newer editions remain in copyright, and PHI reproduces IG texts digitally. The project’s own Digital Scholarly Edition (telota.bbaw.de/ig) gives open access to text, German + English translations, and TEI/EpiDoc source for eleven volumes; project overview at EADH.
  • Digital Classicist Wiki — PHI Greek Inscriptions FREE / PD Concise description of PHI’s scope, history, and coverage.
  • Digital Classicist Wiki — Greek & Latin texts in digital form FREE / PD A broader index of digital text projects, useful for tracking down a specific corpus.
  • SCS review of the PHI Searchable Greek Inscriptions FREE / PD Laura Gawlinski’s practical walkthrough — how to actually search PHI effectively.

2Translations & commentary

  • Attic Inscriptions Online (AIO) CC BY-SA 2,000+ inscriptions of Athens & Attica in annotated English translation, growing steadily. CC BY-SA 3.0 — freely reusable (ShareAlike only bites if you redistribute). The best legal “spine” if you ever build a reader. See also the AIO Papers series and the Digital Classicist entry.
  • Greek Inscriptions Online (GIO) FREE Modern-Greek translations of Ancient Greek inscriptions (8th c. BCE–late antiquity), by the Greek Epigraphic Society and HERC in collaboration with AIO. Browsable by IG reference, region, date, or monument type, with some images. Aimed at Greek students and teachers; free to read (reuse terms not stated).

3Squeeze collections

A squeeze is a paper impression pressed onto the stone — a modern object that records the letterforms in relief. Raked-light photos of squeezes are often easier to read than photos of the stone itself, and US-held collections sit outside Greek heritage-image jurisdiction.

  • Ohio State — Center for Epigraphical & Palaeographical Studies (Greek squeezes) ASK Large collection, mostly from Stephen V. Tracy; Attic and non-Attic. No reuse terms posted — email epig@osu.edu if you ever want to publish. Fine to study/save.
  • Cornell University Squeeze Collection FREE / PD From the 1907–08 Cornell Expedition (incl. a famous early squeeze of the Res Gestae / Monumentum Ancyranum). Digitized 2013; Cornell states the material is believed public domain by age. Browse via Cornell Digital Collections (example record: Erechtheion building inventory).
  • Oxford CSAD Squeeze Collection ASK The Centre for the Study of Ancient Documents’ holdings and digitization work.
  • Krateros — Squeezes of Greek Inscriptions at the IAS FREE The digital repository (on the IAS “Albert” platform) for the Institute for Advanced Study’s collection — ~30,000 squeezes, one of the world’s largest, being published online with NEH funding (current focus: IG II², uploaded in IG-number order). Project home, media, and a useful Pedagogy page at ias.edu/krateros; background on the digitization at the IAS news piece.
  • e-STAMPAGES — digital ectypothèque of Greek squeezes FREE French flagship: ~12,000 Greek inscription squeezes from the École française d’Athènes and HiSoMA (Lyon), with ~6,000 from Delos, Delphi, Thasos, and Philippi given 3D views. The online viewer offers shadow-variation lighting to read the relief — excellent for letterform study. Free consultation; opened 2019. (See also §8, French-language resources.)

4Archaic & epichoric scripts

The single best thread for “various forms”: the local alphabets (red/blue/green types) and dialects before the standard Ionic alphabet. See also §15 for the syllabic scripts (Linear B, Cypriot) and the informal everyday hands.

  • Poinikastas — Epigraphic Sources for Early Greek Writing ASK The digitized L.H. (Anne) Jeffery archive behind Local Scripts of Archaic Greece: ~4,000 drawings and ~600 photographs of archaic epichoric inscriptions. Ideal for reading early letterforms. Project pages: DH@Oxford, CSAD, archive introduction, LSAG introduction.
  • Early Greek Alphabetic Writing (OAPEN) CC Open-access scholarly monograph on the earliest alphabetic writing.
  • Wikipedia — Archaic (epichoric) Greek alphabets CC BY-SA Good orientation to Kirchhoff’s red/blue/green/light-blue classification, with charts.
  • Wikipedia — Greek alphabet CC BY-SA Development of the alphabet, letter-by-letter, including local variants.
  • Nick Nicholas — Epigraphical letterforms & Unicode (PDF) FREE / PD Practical reference for representing epigraphic/archaic letterforms in Unicode — handy if you transcribe digitally.

5Open-access manuals (self-study spine)

Older but public-domain works, mostly on HathiTrust and archive.org. Search by author/title.

  • Carl D. Buck, Introduction to the Study of the Greek Dialects (1910) FREE / PD The closest thing to the book you described: dialect-by-dialect grammar + graded selection of inscriptions + glossary. Your best single model. (archive.org search)
  • E. L. Hicks & G. F. Hill, A Manual of Greek Historical Inscriptions (1901; orig. Hicks 1882) FREE / PD Curated historical inscriptions with interpretive notes and a well-known visual guide to numeral/monetary signs. (archive.org search)
  • E. S. Roberts, An Introduction to Greek Epigraphy (1887–) FREE / PD SUBSCRIPTION Classic teaching manual; Vol. 1 traces the alphabet’s development to the 5th c. BCE. Modern reissue page at Cambridge; original volumes are public domain online.
  • Helen M. Searles, A Lexicographical Study of the Greek Inscriptions (1898) FREE / PD Specialized reference on inscriptional vocabulary.

6Teaching resources & courses

  • Epigraphy Enchiridion (Sarah E. Bond) FREE / PD A curated list of open-access books for teaching Greek & Roman inscriptions — the source for several manuals above. Also reposted at the Athens Centre.
  • UC Berkeley — Greek & Latin Epigraphy (Papazarkadas) FREE / PD Graduate syllabus: alphabet origins, epichoric scripts, making/reading squeezes, dating texts. A model for sequencing topics (though pitched above beginner level).
  • Greek Language & Linguistics — Epigraphy FREE / PD A linguist’s gateway page to epigraphic resources.

7Reference guides & portals

  • EAGLE — Europeana network of Ancient Greek & Latin Epigraphy CC BY-SA Aggregates many epigraphic databases; most images are CC BY-SA. Includes the EDH collection. Background papers: ISAW, Balisage.
  • Epigraphic Database Heidelberg (EDH) CC BY-SA 4.0 Roman-provincial focus (more Latin than Greek), but texts as EpiDoc XML and most images are openly licensed. Bulk data: EpiDoc files on Zenodo; reuse overview: Data Reuse Options; images on Wikimedia Commons.
  • University research guides FREE / PD Curated link-hubs: Penn · Vanderbilt · Catholic Univ. · Hellenic & Roman Library (open access).
  • Supplementum Epigraphicum Graecum (SEG Online) SUBSCRIPTION Annual record of new Greek inscriptions and scholarship (Brill, Leiden). SEG Online adds faceted search by date, place, topic, and type — the standard place to chase current bibliography on any text; institutional access usually needed.
  • British Museum — Classical Inscriptions CC BY-NC-SA FREE / PD Major holdings of Greek inscribed marbles. Search the online collection; most images are released under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 (fine for personal/non-commercial use), with a free high-res image request service. The out-of-copyright catalogue Greek Inscriptions in the British Museum (GIBM, 4 vols, 1874–1916) is on archive.org.
  • Centre for the Study of Ancient Documents (CSAD), Oxford FREE / PD Institutional home for several resources here (Poinikastas, the squeeze collection, LGPN, 3D imaging). Holds one of the world’s largest squeeze collections and hosts the Lexicon of Greek Personal Names and FAIR Epigraphy.
  • Professional societies FREE Field hubs with news, links, and events: the Association Internationale d’Épigraphie Grecque et Latine (AIEGL), the American Society of Greek and Latin Epigraphy (ASGLE), and the British Epigraphy Society — alongside the Greek Epigraphic Society (§10) and SFER (§8).

8French-language resources & institutions

France runs some of the deepest epigraphic infrastructure in the field — especially for the sites the École française d’Athènes has excavated for 150 years (Delphi, Delos, Thasos, Argos…). Much of it is open access.

  • e-STAMPAGES — ectypothèque numérique FREE The flagship (also in §3): ~12,000 Greek squeezes from the EfA and HiSoMA, ~6,000 in 3D (Delos, Delphi, Thasos, Philippi), with a shadow-variation viewer. Built with the University of Florida Digital Epigraphy & Archaeology Project, coordinated by Michèle Brunet.
  • École française d’Athènes (EfA) FREE / PD The institution behind Delphi and Delos. Holds ~6,000 epigraphic estampages plus manuscript and iconographic archives; publisher of Fouilles de Delphes and the Exploration archéologique de Délos. Excavation archives described in Nouvelles de l’archéologie.
  • HiSoMA — Histoire et Sources des Mondes Antiques (Lyon) FREE / PD Major French epigraphy laboratory (Maison de l’Orient et de la Méditerranée); co-runs e-STAMPAGES and the digital edition of IGLS (Inscriptions grecques et latines de la Syrie). Databases pairing texts with photos, squeezes, and field notebooks.
  • Persée FREE / PD Open-access back-runs of core French scholarship: the Bulletin de correspondance hellénique (BCH, vols 1–138, 1877–2014), the BEFAR monograph series, and the Revue des études grecques (which carries the Bulletin épigraphique).
  • OpenEdition Journals — BCH FREE Recent Bulletin de correspondance hellénique issues (from vol. 139) in open access, complementing the Persée back-run above.
  • Bulletin épigraphique (BE) — Association des Études Grecques FREE The indispensable annual critical survey of new Greek epigraphic publications (in the REG). Since 2025 a free, indexed, searchable Bulletin épigraphique numérique covers 2002–2021.
  • SFER — Société Française d’Épigraphie (resources / liens) FREE The French epigraphic society; its resources page is a curated hub of Greek & Latin epigraphy links.
  • Fouilles de Delphes (EfA, public domain volumes) FREE / PD Older excavation volumes, including the epigraphic fascicles, scanned on archive.org.

Also worth knowing: the PETRAE system (Ausonius Institute, Bordeaux) is France’s standard epigraphic-record database, though chiefly Latin/Gaulish material.

9German-language resources & institutions

Germany has anchored Greek epigraphy since the 19th century — both the corpus Inscriptiones Graecae (§1) and the Epigraphic Database Heidelberg (§7) are German projects. A few more:

  • iDAI.objects Arachne FREE The central object & image database of the German Archaeological Institute (DAI) and the University of Cologne — ~4.7 million records including ~2.9 million images (sculpture, objects, and inscribed monuments), free to search. Reuse license varies per image; high-res may need ordering. About.
  • Zeitschrift für Papyrologie und Epigraphik (ZPE) PERSONAL The leading journal of papyrology and epigraphy (Habelt, since 1967). Older volumes (73–133, 1988–2000) are free to download for personal use; newer issues on JSTOR behind a moving wall.
  • Chiron & iDAI.publications (DAI) FREE Chiron (from the DAI’s Kommission für Alte Geschichte und Epigraphik, Munich) and other DAI journals in open access on the iDAI.publications platform.
  • Sammlung der griechischen Dialekt-Inschriften (SGDI) FREE / PD Collitz & Bechtel’s classic corpus of dialect inscriptions (Göttingen, 1884–1915), arranged by region (Cyprus, Aeolic, Thessalian, Boeotian, Arcadian, Delphi…) — directly on point for studying the “various forms.” Digitized at Heidelberg and on archive.org.
  • Propylaeum FREE The German virtual library (specialised information service) for classical studies at UB Heidelberg — a gateway to digitized texts, journals, and databases.

10Greek-language resources & institutions

Resources produced in Greece — several with English interfaces — including the single largest collection of inscriptions anywhere and a teaching portal built around the dialects.

  • Epigraphic Museum, Athens (Επιγραφικό Μουσείο) FREE / PD Founded 1885; holds the largest collection of ancient Greek inscriptions in the world (8th c. BCE–Byzantine, mostly Athens/Attica). English interface. Runs a programme of electronic recording of the inscriptions; for digital images/data contact ema@culture.gr.
  • Ancient Greek Dialects portal (Centre for the Greek Language) FREE A research-and-teaching portal (in Greek) on the ancient dialects: scholarly studies, an Introduction to Greek Epigraphy, an inscriptions section, and bibliographic/epigraphic databases, with special attention to thinly-attested dialects (e.g. Macedonian). One of the best fits for your “various forms” aim.
  • DiGIA — Digitizing Greek Inscriptions & Alphabets FREE Aristotle University of Thessaloniki (Epigraphy & Papyrology Lab): digitized squeezes, inscriptions from Crete and Methoni, documentation of Archaic and Classical Cretan alphabets (with digital fonts), and AR teaching materials. Open-access database.
  • Pandektis (Πανδέκτης) — National Hellenic Research Foundation FREE EIE/NHRF digital collections, including corpora of Greek & Latin inscriptions of Upper Macedonia, Aegean Thrace, and Achaïa — with text, translation, and commentary via the EIE’s Helios repository.
  • Greek Epigraphic Society (Ελληνική Επιγραφική Εταιρεία) FREE Founded 1985; publishes the journal Horos and, since 2012, the freely-available online journal Grammateion (articles on inscriptions, ancient topography, and history).

11Late Roman & Byzantine inscriptions

The corpora above are mostly Classical and Hellenistic. To follow Greek forward into late antiquity and Byzantium — Christian, Late Roman, and medieval Greek — these are the specialists, and were the payoff of the Italian and Turkish searches. Many Asia Minor texts also live in the German Inschriften griechischer Städte aus Kleinasien (IK) series and in PHI.

  • Byzantine epigraphy — handbooks & introductions FREE Orientation to the field, mostly from Andreas Rhoby (ÖAW Vienna): the open-access Inscriptions in Byzantium and Beyond: Methods – Projects – Case Studies (2015) — the first collective volume on the subject — whose opening chapter, “A Short History of Byzantine Epigraphy,” is a free standalone PDF; and Studies in Byzantine Epigraphy (Brepols, 2023–, some chapters open via Oxford ORA). Rhoby’s multi-volume Byzantinische Epigramme edits the Byzantine verse inscriptions (some volumes on archive.org). The comprehensive Handbook of Byzantine Epigraphy (Rhoby & Toth) is announced but not yet published — one to watch.
  • Inscriptiones Christianae Graecae (ICG) FREE partly CC The key database for early Christian Greek inscriptions of Asia Minor and Greece (ca. 2nd–6th c. CE): 4,700+ inscriptions, 6,500+ images, each with Greek text, German/English translation, apparatus, and commentary. Kiel University (from the Topoi cluster); a base version is open access via edition Topoi.
  • Aphrodisias in Late Antiquity (ALA2004) / Inscriptions of Aphrodisias (IAph) CC BY Charlotte Roueché’s electronic corpus of ~1,600 inscriptions from Aphrodisias (SW Turkey), Hellenistic through Late Antique, richly annotated in EpiDoc. The flagship for Anatolian late-Roman/Byzantine epigraphy; hosted at INSAPH (King’s College London).
  • Epigraphic Database Roma (EDR) FREE Greek and Latin inscriptions of Italy, Sardinia, and Sicily through the 7th c. CE (71,000+ texts, with images) — excluding the Christian ones covered by EDB. An EAGLE founding database.
  • Epigraphic Database Bari (EDB) FREE Inscriptions by Christians in Rome, 3rd–8th c. CE — a digital, updated Inscriptiones Christianae Urbis Romae (~50,000 texts), with links to images held by the Pontifical Commission for Sacred Archaeology.
  • I.Sicily CC BY Open digital corpus of the inscriptions of Sicily across all periods and languages (Greek, Latin, Oscan, Punic…), including late-antique and Byzantine material. EpiDoc, with images.
  • Iscrizioni Greche d’Italia (IGI) PRINT The modern corpus replacing the Italian portion of IG XIV: full editions with photographs and commentary, region by region (Edizioni Quasar). Primarily print, but the authoritative reference for Greek Italy.
  • F. Fiori, Epigrafi greche dell’Italia bizantina (VII–XI sec.) FREE / PD Open-access study (Bologna, AMS Acta) of Greek inscriptions from Byzantine Italy — a focused way into the medieval Greek of the peninsula. Compare A. Guillou, Recueil des inscriptions grecques médiévales d’Italie.
  • LIBRI — Epigrafi, Çeviri ve Eleştiri Dergisi FREE Turkish open-access review journal on epigraphy, translation, and criticism — a window onto epigraphic scholarship on Anatolia (which holds a vast share of Greek inscriptions).
  • Kayıp Diller (“Lost Languages”) FREE A Turkish public-humanities site with accessible exhibitions on the coming of writing to Anatolia and on Byzantine / medieval Greek — useful orientation material, in Turkish.
  • DBMNT — Database of Medieval Nubian Texts FREE 4,500+ texts from Christian Nubia (6th–15th c. CE) in Greek, Coptic, and Old Nubian — epitaphs and inscriptions with transcriptions and translations (University of Warsaw). Greek persisted as a liturgical and epigraphic language on the Middle Nile long after the Arab conquest — the southern edge of the Byzantine Greek world.

12Regional & bilingual corpora (Greek-inclusive)

Region-specific corpora — some wholly Greek, some mixing Greek with Latin (and at times Hebrew, Aramaic, or Etruscan). Purely Latin databases (CIL, Clauss-Slaby/EDCS, and the like) are deliberately left out.

  • IOSPE — Ancient Inscriptions of the Northern Black Sea CC A modern online edition of ~4,000 predominantly Greek inscriptions from Tyras, Olbia, Chersonesos, and the Bosporan kingdom, c. 600 BCE–1400 CE — with a whole Byzantine volume. EpiDoc, richly indexed (KCL). Excellent for both the Classical and Byzantine ends of your range.
  • Inscriptions of Israel/Palestine (IIP) CC BY-NC 4,000+ inscriptions in Greek, Latin, Hebrew, and Aramaic, c. 6th c. BCE–7th c. CE — a large, Greek-rich late-antique corpus. CC BY-NC 4.0; XML downloadable individually, in bulk from GitHub, or via API (Brown).
  • CIIP — Corpus Inscriptionum Iudaeae/Palaestinae PRINT The authoritative multilingual corpus (Greek, Latin, Hebrew, Aramaic, Syriac, Armenian) of all inscriptions of Israel/Palestine from Alexander to the Arab conquest — Vol. I is Jerusalem, covering the Byzantine city before 638 CE. Print (De Gruyter); some volumes scanned on archive.org. The print counterpart to the digital IIP above.
  • Israel Antiquities Authority (IAA) FREE Israel’s statutory archaeology body: no single Greek-inscriptions database, but the institution behind new finds, with an online services portal, digital archives, the National Library for Archaeology, and the excavation journal Hadashot Arkheologiyot — where Byzantine mosaic inscriptions regularly surface. Restored church pavements are displayed at the Israel Museum.
  • U.S. Epigraphy Project (USEP) FREE ~3,500 Greek and Latin inscriptions held in U.S. collections (ca. 800 BCE–700 CE), with ~1,000 images and ~300 paper squeezes of Greek inscriptions from Attica and Asia Minor (Brown). Browse or search by language, date, origin, and material.
  • Telamon — Ancient Greek Inscriptions from Bulgaria FREE Sofia University’s bilingual digital corpus of 4,000+ Greek inscriptions from ancient Thrace and Moesia (6th c. BCE–6th c. CE), revising Mihailov’s Inscriptiones Graecae in Bulgaria repertae (IGBulg) and Beševliev’s late-antique corpus, with translations, images, and search — Classical through early Byzantine.
  • IGCyr & GVCyr — Inscriptions of Greek Cyrenaica FREE ~950 inscriptions plus ~59 verse inscriptions from Cyrenaica (eastern Libya), TEI-EpiDoc, open access and multilingual (English, French, Italian, Arabic), with the fullest available images, squeezes, and drawings (Bologna). Thoroughly Greek.
  • Archaeological Museum of North Macedonia — Epigraphy & Stone Sculpture ASK The main institutional collection for ancient Macedonia and Paeonia (Greek and Latin inscriptions; English interface). No dedicated open text corpus — much of the Greek material is edited in IG X (Macedonia) and, for Upper Macedonia, in the EAM corpus available through Pandektis (§10).
  • CIGIME — Inscriptions grecques d’Illyrie méridionale et d’Épire FREE Cabanes et al.’s corpus of the Greek inscriptions of Albania and Epirus — Apollonia, Epidamnos-Dyrrhachion, Bouthrotos, and Molossia. Print (École française d’Athènes), with older volumes readable through the EfA’s open digital library CEFAEL. The standard route into Greek Illyria; there is no native Albanian database.
  • IGLS — Inscriptions grecques et latines de la Syrie FREE The corpus of Greek & Latin inscriptions of ancient Syria — today’s Syria, Lebanon, and Jordan (including the Inscriptions de la Jordanie sub-series). Run from Lyon (HiSoMA) and Beirut (Ifpo); an EpiDoc edition is under way, and the ~20 published volumes are free on Persée. Rich in late-antique/Byzantine church inscriptions. (The digital infrastructure is French/IFPO, not Syrian.)
  • CPI — Corpus of Ptolemaic Inscriptions FREE Oxford CSAD’s online edition of ~550 Greek, bilingual, and trilingual stone inscriptions of Ptolemaic Egypt (332–30 BCE), with introductions, commentary, and images; all three volumes online (print by OUP).
  • Bibliotheca Alexandrina — Antiquities Museum FREE An Egyptian institution’s digital catalogue (Arabic / French / English) whose largest holdings are Greco-Roman; searchable, with images. Object-focused rather than a text corpus, but a rare openly-browsable Egyptian collection that includes inscribed material.
  • Greek Inscriptions in Georgia (T. Qaukhchishvili) ASK The standard corpus of Greek inscriptions found in Georgia (Colchis & Iberia), digitized in Georgian university repositories (with a related bilingual monograph on TSU dSpace); access to individual scans may be limited, and there is no open interactive database.
  • EDAK — Epigraphic Database for Ancient Asia Minor FREE Hamburg’s database of the Greek & Latin inscriptions of Asia Minor — 6,000+ so far from Lydia, Galatia, Paphlagonia, Phrygia, and Proseilemmene, with descriptions, translations, and commentary. TEI/EpiDoc, filterable, downloadable as JSON/CSV. Fills the biggest Greek zone we had only touched via Aphrodisias.
  • MAMA XI — Monumenta Asiae Minoris Antiqua FREE Oxford CSAD’s open edition of 387 monuments (292 previously unpublished) from Phrygia and Lykaonia, from the 1950s Calder–Ballance expeditions, with high-resolution photographs of every piece and an emphasis on the Roman and Early Byzantine periods.

Thinner or partial coverage worth knowing: for Egypt, no dedicated open epigraphic database surfaced at the American University in Cairo or the Egyptian Museum — Greek Egypt is reached mainly through CPI (above), Trismegistos (see Tools — its heartland), and the Bernand editions within PHI. For Jordan/Nabataea, see the open-access Corpus of Nabataean Aramaic-Greek Inscriptions (Ca’ Foscari) and the Jordan Journal for History and Archaeology. In the Balkans, Prometheus pairs inscriptions with interactive maps and audio; and Romanian Scythia Minor (Histria, Tomis, Callatis) is served by the print ISM and PHI’s Scythia Minor region — mixed Greek/Latin, no dedicated open Greek corpus.

13Adjacent field: Greek papyri

Papyri are a different medium from stone, but they preserve everyday and cursive Greek hands — a further set of “forms” of the written language. The essentials (the net-new from the CUA guide, which is otherwise papyrology or Latin epigraphy we already cover):

  • Papyri.info CC The central aggregator — the Duke Databank of Documentary Papyri (DDbDP), HGV, and APIS searchable together, with EpiDoc texts. First stop for documentary Greek papyri.
  • POxy: Oxyrhynchus Online FREE The Oxyrhynchus finds — literary and documentary papyri, searchable by publication number, author, genre, and date, with images.
  • Papyrus Portal & national collections FREE Gateway to the German papyrus collections; comparable image collections exist at Heidelberg, Leipzig/Halle/Jena, the Sorbonne, and (via CSAD) the Cairo Museum papyri. Trismegistos (see Tools) is the natural bridge between papyri and inscriptions.

14Low Countries & Nordic scholarship hubs

As you anticipated, these are mostly scholarship — publishers, institutes, and teaching portals — rather than find-spots, since the region lay in the Latin West. A few are indispensable even so.

  • Brill & SEG Online (Leiden) SUBSCRIPTION Brill, the Leiden publisher (founded 1683), issues the Supplementum Epigraphicum Graecum — the annual survey of new Greek inscriptions (archaic–8th c. CE), with faceted search by date, place, topic, and type (cross-ref §7). Brill also publishes CIIP (§12) and much of the corpus literature.
  • KU Leuven — Trismegistos & LDAB FREE Leuven (Belgium) is home to Trismegistos (the cross-database identifier hub — see Tools) and the Leuven Database of Ancient Books (LDAB), an overview of all literary manuscripts of antiquity to 800 CE.
  • Ghent — Digitaal Platform Historische Praktijk FREE A Dutch-language teaching/heuristics portal (Ghent University) with practical pages on Greek inscriptions, the databases, and the tools of the trade — a good methods primer if you read Dutch. KU Leuven has a parallel epigraphy / papyrology / palaeography guide.
  • Swedish Institute at Athens & Opuscula FREE A foreign school in Greece (est. 1946) whose excavations produced Greek-inscription corpora — notably Crampa’s Labraunda: The Greek Inscriptions — published in Opuscula and the Acta series (ecsi.se), largely open access.

Denmark is thin for Greek epigraphy specifically: Copenhagen’s strengths are the P.Haun papyri (§13) and a world-class Greek coin cabinet, not an inscriptions corpus; Danish and Aarhus scholarship reaches Greek inscriptions through the international databases already listed.

15Other scripts & genres of written Greek

Greek was written in far more than the monumental stone-cut alphabet — on clay, pottery, lead, metal, and plaster. These corpora cover the forms most unlike the standard lapidary script: the earliest syllabic Greek, a parallel syllabary, the informal everyday hands, the miniature legends of coins and seals, and scratched graffiti — exactly the “various forms” you're after.

  • DAMOS — Database of Mycenaean at Oslo (Linear B) FREE The annotated corpus of all published Mycenaean texts — the earliest Greek (c. 15th–12th c. BCE), in the Linear B syllabary. Full transliterations, metadata, and epigraphical/morphological/syntactic annotation with word search (University of Oslo). Pair with LiBER (CNR) for the tablets of Mycenae, Tiryns, and Midea with photographs and apparatus.
  • Kyprios Character — Cypriot syllabic inscriptions FREE The National Hellenic Research Foundation’s searchable corpus of 1,300+ inscriptions in the Cypriot syllabary — the non-alphabetic script that wrote Greek on Cyprus into the Hellenistic period. Builds on Masson’s ICS; the print corpus continues as IG XV.
  • AVI — Attic Vase Inscriptions FREE Basel’s database of ~8,000 inscriptions painted or scratched on Attic vases (extending Immerwahr’s CAVI): potter/painter signatures, mythological labels, kalos praises, “nonsense” strings, and speech captions — the informal, archaic-lettered end of Greek writing. Linked to the Beazley Archive.
  • TheDefix — Thesaurus Defixionum FREE An open corpus of ancient curse tablets (Greek katadesmoi, Latin defixiones), 5th c. BCE–5th c. CE, with texts, images, and bibliography (Hamburg; successor to TheDeMa). Cursive, colloquial, often magical Greek on lead — a very different register.
  • Byzantine coins & lead seals — Dumbarton Oaks FREE The world’s foremost collection (~17,500 coins + ~17,000 seals), with open online catalogues searchable by legend, issuer, mint, date, and metal, plus images. Byzantine coin legends and seal inscriptions are Greek (increasingly so from the 7th c.) — a vast, closely dated epigraphic corpus. Digital seal editions abound: SigiDoc / Zacos (Paris; TEI-XML for seals), DigiByzSeal (Cologne; ~4,000 seals, open source), the ÖAW sigillography group (Vienna), and PBW (prosopography built largely from seals). Coin-find contexts: Nomismata (Trieste).
  • Greek & Byzantine graffiti MIXED The scratched, informal register. Epigraphies of Pious Travel (ÖAW Vienna / HSE) is editing the Greek pilgrim graffiti of the eastern Mediterranean (c. 300–1500 CE) into a forthcoming open OpenAtlas database. The richest single hoard is the Basilica of Smyrna (published by Bagnall et al., ISAW/NYU — print). See also the print Corpus of Greek Graffiti from Dalmatia, the medieval graffiti of St Sophia in Kyiv (Vysotsky), and a digital corpus of painted Greek (Byzantine) inscriptions from Cyprus.

16Editorial conventions & digital tools

How editions encode damage, restorations, and abbreviations — you must read these fluently to use any corpus.

  • The Leiden Conventions CC BY-SA The bracket system: [ ] restoration, ( ) expanded abbreviation, < > editorial addition, dots for uncertain letters, etc. Learn this first.
  • EpiDoc FREE / PD The TEI-XML standard for digital epigraphic editions (used by AIO, EDH, and others). Relevant if you want to keep your transcriptions in a structured, portable format.
  • Ithaca & Pythia (Google DeepMind + Oxford/Venice) OPEN SOURCE Deep-learning tools that restore missing text and help date and localize Greek inscriptions, trained on ~78,000 texts from PHI. Open source on GitHub, with an interactive demo at ithaca.deepmind.com; background from DeepMind. A curiosity and aid, not a crutch.
  • Trismegistos FREE A cross-database hub of stable identifiers linking the same inscription or text across the major corpora — handy for chasing one document through PHI, EDH, and others.
