Two routes, one destination. There are genuinely two ways to get to Byzantine Greek, and they converge. The traditional route goes Ancient Greek → Koine/Biblical Greek → Byzantine Greek via a university course that assumes you already read Ancient Greek (this is how UCLouvain's "Grec byzantin" and most university courses work — see our earlier research). The direct route skips straight to Byzantine Greek with a course built for true beginners, using Byzantine texts themselves as the teaching material from day one. I've built this plan around both, with the direct route as the headline recommendation since it's the fastest way to your actual goal, and the traditional route kept as a parallel option since it's more widely available and dovetails with everything we found for the PhD list.

Stage 0 — Ancient (Attic) Greek foundations (optional — skip if taking the direct route)

Needed only if you want to go the traditional route, or if you'd simply like Attic literature to be readable too. Several university Byzantine Greek courses (UCLouvain among them) list this as a hard prerequisite.
English-medium textbooks
Commercial Athenaze: An Introduction to Ancient Greek (Balme, Lawall, Miraglia) — the most widely used current course; continuous narrative, gentle progression
Commercial Reading Greek (JACT / Cambridge University Press) — similarly reading-led, one-year intensive course
Free lsj.gr's "Learn Ancient Greek" page and Textkit.com — both aggregate dozens of pre-1923 public-domain grammars (Goodwin, White's First Greek Book, Pharr's Homeric Greek, etc.) as free PDFs
French-medium textbook FR
Commercial Jean-Victor Vernhes, Ἕρμαιον (Hermaion): Initiation au grec ancien (Ophrys) — the standard contemporary French introductory textbook, widely used in French universities; pairs naturally with UCLouvain-style programs later on

Stage 1 — The Koine bridge

Worth doing even if you're taking the direct route: Koine is the actual historical starting point of Byzantine Greek (see our first conversation on this), the grammar is genuinely simpler than Attic, and the free material is abundant. This is also literally what several university programs pair with Byzantine Greek (e.g. UCLouvain's "Grec biblique et byzantin" track runs NT/OT Greek alongside Byzantine Greek).
Grammar
Free Archive.org — J. Gresham Machen, New Testament Greek for Beginners (1923)
Free Textkit.com — F.C. Conybeare & St. George Stock, Grammar of Septuagint Greek (1905) — useful since LXX Greek is even closer in register to a lot of Byzantine prose than the NT is
Commercial William Mounce, Basics of Biblical Greek, or N. Clayton Croy, A Primer of Biblical Greek — the two most commonly used current classroom textbooks, if you'd rather have something modern and in print
Reading texts
Free SBLGNT — a full critically-edited Greek New Testament, free under CC-BY 4.0, the obvious first reading text GR
Once you can read the Gospels and Acts with reasonable fluency, you already have real traction on a large fraction of Byzantine vocabulary and syntax — biblical language is the deepest substrate of the whole tradition, quoted and echoed constantly by Byzantine authors of every register.

Stage 2 — Orientation reading (do this in parallel with Stage 0/1 or Stage 3)

Not a language course — a conceptual map of what you're walking into, so the grammar you're learning has a historical home. Short investment, high payoff.
The two essential overviews
Commercial Geoffrey Horrocks, Greek: A History of the Language and its Speakers (2nd ed., Wiley-Blackwell) — read Part 2 ("Byzantium") specifically
Commercial Robert Browning, Medieval and Modern Greek (2nd ed., Cambridge UP) — shorter, older, still a clean survey
Free Cambridge Grammar of Medieval and Early Modern Greek — General Introduction (free PDF) — explains the diglossia, periodization, and source-text methodology that everything downstream depends on

Stage 3 — First contact with Byzantine Greek KEY

This is the centerpiece of the whole plan: a real, current, structured course that teaches Byzantine Greek as itself, from the alphabet up, with no Ancient Greek required.
International Byzantine Greek Summer School (IBGSS) — Trinity College Dublin
Course tcd.ie/classics/byzantine — €375 for two weeks, runs online, small groups
Level 1 (Beginners) — explicitly "designed for those with no previous knowledge of Greek." Two weeks, two 2-hour sessions daily. Starts with the alphabet, covers noun/adjective declensions, core pronouns, and the indicative/infinitive/participial forms of present and aorist, with a taste of future, perfect, and subjunctive. Students read real Byzantine text extracts from day one with full vocabulary support, and finish able to translate simple passages with a dictionary. Uses Modern Greek pronunciation throughout (closer to actual Byzantine pronunciation than the Erasmian system used for Classical Greek).

Level 2/2.5 (Intermediate/Higher Intermediate) — assumes Level 1 or equivalent (a school/university Ancient Greek course counts). Completes basic grammar (future, perfect, subjunctive, optative) and moves into genre reading: selections from Theophanes' Chronographia, the Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom, and a saint's life are the typical texts — note Theophanes is also on the Harvard PhD reading list we built, so this is direct preparation for it.

Level 3 (Advanced Reading) — no fixed syllabus; students propose texts and the course reads widely across genres, registers, and periods (late Byzantine vernacular included), with optional palaeography sessions.
Directed by Dr Anthony Hirst, running continuously since 2002 (Belfast → Birmingham → Dublin). 2026 dates: Level 1, 20–31 July; Levels 2/2.5 and 3, 3–14 August.

If you can't attend IBGSS

Self-study substitute: do Stage 0 + Stage 1 thoroughly (Attic or Koine grammar gets you most of the morphology IBGSS Level 1 covers), then jump straight into Stage 4's graded readings below, leaning hard on the bilingual texts. It's a slower, lonelier version of the same path, but it works.

A complementary, non-required track: Modern Greek

Because Byzantine pronunciation is essentially Modern Greek pronunciation (as IBGSS itself teaches), and because the vernacular texts on the PhD list (Digenes Akrites, Ptochoprodromos, the Lament for Constantinople) read far more transparently to a Modern Greek speaker than to an Atticist, some learners find real value in parallel basic Modern Greek study — useful but optional, and not a substitute for the grammar work above.

Stage 4 — Graded Byzantine readings (build confidence before the heavy texts)

Short, accessible, free, bilingual-where-possible texts to read once you have basic morphology under control (post-IBGSS Level 1, or post-Stage 0/1 if self-studying). Several of these are entries on the PhD reading list itself — so this stage doubles as early, gentle contact with material you'll need anyway.
Life of Mary of Egypt (Sophronios of Jerusalem)
Free Full Greek text, Holy Metropolis of Morfou GR
A classic "first real text" choice in Byzantine Greek pedagogy: straightforward narrative hagiography, simple syntax, vivid plot (desert hermit meets a former prostitute turned ascetic), no dense theology to wade through. Not on the PhD list itself, but a gentle warm-up for the hagiographic register that is on it (Leontios of Neapolis, Symeon Metaphrastes).
Akathistos Hymn
Free Bilingual Greek–English edition, archive.org GR
Highly repetitive refrain structure ("Χαῖρε...") makes it unusually approachable for an early reader. Core item on the PhD list — you'll have already read it.
Lament for Constantinople (Ανακάλημα της Κωνσταντινόπολης)
Free Full text, Centre for the Greek Language GR
Only 118 lines, vernacular register (closer to Modern Greek than to Atticizing prose), vivid narrative. Also on the PhD list.
Romanos Melodos — selected Kontakia
Free Greek text (Pitra) GR + English translation (Carpenter)
Poetic but liturgical/narrative rather than rhetorically dense; many kontakia retell familiar biblical stories, so Stage 1's Gospel reading pays off directly here. On the PhD list.
Digenes Akrites
Free Full bilingual Greek–English edition (Mavrogordato) GR
Longer and more demanding than the others here, but the facing English translation makes it usable as a graded reader even before you're fully independent — and it's the one "core, no-substitution" text on the PhD list, so building familiarity early is worth it.

Stage 5 — A real university-level Byzantine Greek course

Once Stage 3/4 is solid, this is where you plug into the academic courses we researched earlier in this conversation.
English-medium
Course IBGSS Levels 2/2.5 and 3 (above) — designed to be returned to year after year
Course Dumbarton Oaks Greek Summer School — graduate-level, prerequisite of 2 years college Ancient Greek; reads a different genre each week (historiography, hagiography, poetry, epistolography) plus palaeography
French-medium FR
Course UCLouvain's LGLOR2791/2792 "Grec byzantin: langue et textes A/B" (Véronique Somers) — full detail in our earlier research; requires prior Ancient Greek
Course Sorbonne Université's UFR de Grec, or Université de Strasbourg's distance-learning DU (includes "Grec patristique," open to external students) — both covered earlier
